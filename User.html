Human Milk Bank

A human milk bank, also known as a breast milk bank or lactarium, is a service where donated breast milk from nursing mothers is collected, screened, processed, pasteurized, and then given to babies who need it, based on a doctor's prescription. Breast milk is the best nutrition for newborns for at least the first six months.For mothers who can't breastfeed or produce enough milk, pasteurized donor breast milk can be a good alternative.

However, using breast milk from another woman that hasn't been pasteurized and sharing breast milk informally can be risky, as it may carry bacteria and viruses that could harm the baby. Why Breast Milk Is Important For Babies?

Breastfeeding is crucial for babies because it provides them with the ideal nutrition they need for healthy growth and development. Breast milk is uniquely tailored to a baby’s needs, containing the perfect balance of nutrients such as proteins, fats, vitamins, and minerals. These nutrients are essential for building strong bones, muscles, and a healthy brain.

One of the key benefits of breastfeeding is that it strengthens a baby’s immune system. Breast milk is rich in antibodies that help protect babies from infections and diseases, such as colds, ear infections, and digestive issues. Babies who are breastfed tend to get sick less often and recover more quickly when they do.

Breast milk is also gentle on a baby’s developing digestive system. It is easier to digest than formula, which can reduce the risk of stomach problems like constipation, gas, and colic. Additionally, the composition of breast milk changes over time to meet the growing needs of the baby, providing exactly what the baby needs at each stage of development.

Why Milk Donation Is Importatnt?

Breast milk donation is important because it provides essential nutrition to newborns, especially those who are born early, too small, or sick. Breast milk has all the right nutrients, antibodies, and enzymes that help babies grow strong, develop well, and fight off infections.

For babies in neonatal intensive care units (NICUs) or whose mothers can’t produce enough milk, donated breast milk is often the safest and healthiest option instead of formula.

By donating breast milk, you also help mothers who can’t breastfeed due to health issues or complications after giving birth. This ensures that their babies still get the best possible nutrition.

Additionally, breast milk donation can help lower the risk of infant deaths and improve the long-term health of babies who need extra care. When you donate to milk banks, you play an important role in giving these babies a better start in life and making a positive impact on their health.



Who is Eligible for Milk Donation?

Eligibility for breast milk donation generally includes healthy mothers who produce more milk than their baby needs. Donors should be non-smokers, free from certain medications, and not using drugs or alcohol. They must have a healthy lifestyle and undergo screening to ensure they don't have infections like HIV, hepatitis, or other illnesses that could be passed through breast milk.

Some milk banks may also require donors to follow specific dietary guidelines and avoid consuming large amounts of caffeine or herbal supplements. The screening process helps ensure the safety and quality of the donated breast milk for vulnerable infants.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is donated breast milk processed and screened?

At Babyfeed we follow a rigorous protocol when it comes to the processing and screening of donor milk, and adhere to stringent quality and safety benchmarks. The milk is carefully screened, pasteurized and stored to feed the babies in times of emergency.

Who can donate breast milk to a human milk bank?

Eligible donors for a human milk bank are healthy, lactating mothers who have surplus breast milk and meet specific health and lifestyle criteria, including being non-smokers and free from certain medications or infections. They are typically screened through interviews and blood tests.

Is donor milk as beneficial as a mother's own milk?

Donor milk is highly beneficial and provides essential nutrients and antibodies, but a mother's own milk is generally preferred because it is tailored to her baby's specific needs. However, donor milk is the next best option for infants when a mother's milk is unavailable.

What conditions can prevent a mother from breastfeeding her baby?

Conditions that can prevent a mother from breastfeeding include certain infections (like HIV), some medications, and health issues such as untreated tuberculosis or undergoing chemotherapy. Physical challenges like insufficient milk supply or certain breast surgeries can also be factors.

Can a woman be a milk donor if she smokes or drinks?

No, a woman can not be a milk donor if she drinks or smokes.
